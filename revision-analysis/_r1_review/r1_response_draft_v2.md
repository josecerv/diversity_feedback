## New prose block (for the response letter)

> **RESPONSE:** Thank you for raising this alternative. We agree it is worth testing directly, and we report the follow-up analysis below (Table R1). We have also added a footnote to the Discussion of Study 4B (p. X) that summarizes this evidence and makes the underlying theoretical claim more explicit in the manuscript.
>
> In our studies we assume that participants do not enter the task with a normative or "appropriate" level ex ante for any attribute in the absence of explicit goals or prescriptions; we anticipate that the activation of a representational concern occurs only after participants receive descriptive feedback, and only for race and gender. This is consistent with the theoretical framing already in the paper: descriptive feedback shifts behavior on a given dimension only when that dimension carries an implicit injunctive norm against unequal treatment, which we have argued holds for race and gender but not for the comparison attributes we test (see Introduction, p. X, and General Discussion, p. X).
>
> That said, we can examine directly what happens when feedback flags zero representation of the focal attribute. Among participants whose initial selection contained zero of an attribute, the inference that the attribute is "missing" from the initial set could itself produce some directional motivation to select one of those candidates next, independent of any injunctive norm; if our race/gender feedback effects were driven by this inference rather than by an underlying norm, we would expect comparable feedback effects on the comparison attributes among the subset of participants who picked zero of them initially. We do not observe that. Pooling participants across Studies 2, 3A, 3B, 4A, and 4B in a study-by-attribute fixed-effects regression with participant-clustered standard errors, restricted to the participant-attribute observations in which the initial selection contained zero of the focal attribute, race/gender feedback increases the probability of selecting a candidate with the focal attribute by +21.28 percentage points (*p* < .0001, *N* = 1,222 across all five studies), while comparison-attribute feedback increases that probability by only +6.83 percentage points, which is not statistically distinguishable from zero (*p* = .21, *N* = 429 across six attribute-by-study cells; the remaining nine cells are omitted because the comparison attribute is common in its candidate pool and almost no participant picked zero of it initially). A Wald test of the difference between the two pooled coefficients yields +14.46 percentage points (*p* = .016, 95% CI [+2.70, +26.21]).
>
> Table R1 reports each per-cell estimate together with the pooled coefficients and the Wald-test difference. The first column lists each study and its total sample size; the second column reports the race or gender (target) feedback effect among participants whose initial selection contained zero of the target attribute; the third column reports the analogous effect for each estimable comparison attribute in that study. The bottom two rows give the pooled fixed-effects coefficients for target and comparison feedback, respectively, and the final row reports the Wald-test difference between them. We acknowledge that the comparison side is estimable for only six of the fifteen comparison attribute-by-study cells, and we therefore present this as a directional, lower-powered test. The new survey described below complements this behavioral evidence by asking participants directly about the appropriateness of different attribute mixes.

## Draft footnote for the Study 4B Discussion (manuscript-side)

> ⁿ Consistent with the account that descriptive feedback operates by surfacing an implicit injunctive norm, a complementary analysis pooled across Studies 2, 3A, 3B, 4A, and 4B shows that race/gender feedback continues to shift the final selection even among participants whose initial selection contained zero of the focal attribute (pooled +21.28 percentage points, *p* < .0001, *N* = 1,222), whereas comparison-attribute feedback does not (pooled +6.83 percentage points, *p* = .21, *N* = 429 across the six attribute-by-study cells in which a zero-initial subgroup is estimable; the remaining nine cells are omitted because the attribute is common in its candidate pool and almost no participant picked zero of it initially). A Wald test of the difference between the two pooled coefficients yields +14.46 percentage points (*p* = .016, 95% CI [+2.70, +26.21]). This pattern is consistent with our broader claim that descriptive feedback shifts behavior on a given dimension only when participants carry an implicit injunctive norm about that dimension that the feedback can surface, which we observe for race and gender but not for the comparison attributes we test. Per-cell estimates are reported in Table R1 of the response letter.
