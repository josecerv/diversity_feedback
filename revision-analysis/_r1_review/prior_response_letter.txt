Dear Dr. Wu,
We thank you and the Associate Editor on this manuscript for providing valuable feedback on our paper and for sending it to three extremely helpful reviewers. We sincerely appreciate the advice provided and the opportunity to revise and resubmit this manuscript. In response to the helpful feedback we’ve received, we have undertaken a major revision of the paper, and we hope you will agree that the changes we’ve made have improved the manuscript substantially.
Here is a summary of the most notable changes we’ve made to our paper:
We have clarified the scope of our theorizing and empirics. We focus specifically on diversity-related feedback – that is, feedback about the race and gender composition of past selectees – as this is a widely-used organizational practice we explore both theoretically and empirically in our paper. We now explain that our focus is on understanding whether and why such feedback may increase the representation of women and racial minorities in organizations. The rationale for distinguishing the effects of this type of feedback from the effects of feedback about other (non-diversity-related) features of people under consideration for opportunities is also better articulated in our revised manuscript: we include other types of feedback to establish that our findings (a) do not arise due to demand effects and (b) do not always arise when any type of feedback is provided.
To better clarify the organizational relevance of our work, we have revised our introduction to offer examples of the practice we study in high profile organizational settings (e.g., at National Public Radio (NPR), the British Broadcasting Corporation (BBC) and Salesforce), and we have collected new data situated in more organizationally relevant contexts. For instance, our new Study 1 simulates the workflow of NPR producers selecting experts for news stories, mirroring the real system used by NPR to provide its journalists with feedback about the demographics of who they have chosen to feature.
We have ruled out the possibility that attribute importance explains our effect by collecting new data (including a new Study 4B). In this study, participants in a pre-test rated the importance of numerous author attributes including an author’s gender, and we chose several other attributes that were rated just as important as author gender to include in a set of stimuli involving the selection of authors to be featured in a library reading list.  Those other items were whether an author wrote works spanning multiple genres, whether they had at least one book with 30 million or more copies sold, and whether they had any work in continuous print for >50 years. In this study, we find that providing feedback about the gender composition of an initially-selected portfolio of authors still produces significantly larger effects on subsequent author selections than providing feedback about these other, equally important author attributes. This establishes that our effect is not simply due to gender’s perceived importance as an attribute.
We address the concern that our effects may be driven by a particular study paradigm by collecting new data using an entirely different experimental paradigm (see Study 1), and we replicate our findings using this new paradigm. Specifically, rather than having participants make their own initial selections (as in our other studies), in Study 1 we asked participants to imagine they were NPR producers who had already selected 10 experts to appear on NPR over the past three months. We then provided feedback about the attributes of their (hypothetical) past guest selections before asking them to select new experts to feature in the future. Additionally, unlike our other studies where participants selected a single final candidate for an opportunity, participants in Study 1 selected three experts simultaneously to feature in upcoming stories, and our dependent variable was the proportion of women selected (rather than the binary outcome used in our other studies: whether a woman (or underrepresented minority candidate) was selected). Our key findings replicate in Study 1. We find that providing feedback about the proportion of past selectees who were women increases the subsequent selection of women experts; and this kind of feedback is significantly more potent at changing future selection decisions than feedback about other expert attributes (e.g., whether the expert worked at a university).
We have revised our introduction to provide a more thorough review of the social norms and goal setting literatures and to more clearly articulate the novelty of our contribution and how it builds on and extends prior work.  Specifically, we posit that feedback that highlights the underrepresentation of women or racial minorities signals a potential violation of the implicit injunctive norm that prejudice on the basis of race and gender is unacceptable, which triggers both internal (self-image) and external (reputational) concerns that motivate behavior change.
We have further clarified when and why our effect appears in different settings. We re-ran our previous Study 3 (now Study 5) in order to explore the mechanisms driving how descriptive feedback about the gender of past selectees affects selection decisions when women are overrepresented versus underrepresented. When women are underrepresented, we replicate our usual effect: feedback increases the selection of women. But when women are overrepresented, the effect is significantly smaller in magnitude and reverses in direction (feedback decreases the selection of women). In exploratory analyses, we determine that when women are overrepresented and people receive feedback suggesting they have overrepresented women in a group, they are more likely to choose to add a man to the group due to fairness concerns (rather than the concerns about prejudice that we theorize propel decisions in response to feedback suggesting women or racial minorities have been underrepresented). We hope you will agree that these new data and analyses strengthen our work and offer a clearer explanation for how the phenomenon we study manifests across different candidate pool compositions. 
Below we reproduce your letter and each of the reviews in their entirety, and we respond to every point made. We have provided normal text from the review team and our responses appear in bolded font for ease of differentiation.

RESPONSE: Thank you for raising this valuable point about whether our results simply reflect the inherent importance of diversity.
When designing our experiments, we aimed to select comparison attributes that would be highly relevant and important to each specific decision task. For example, in Study 3B (previously Study 1A), where participants selected biographical films for a Facebook advertising campaign, we included attributes central to film evaluation: budget (distinguishing blockbusters from independent films), year of release (indicating contemporary relevance), and the protagonist’s profession (capturing the film’s core material). 
However, we understand that our intuitions about the importance of attributes may not perfectly map onto participants’ perceptions, so in response to the review team’s feedback, we conducted a new study (Study 4B) designed after empirically validating the importance of the attributes it would include. Specifically, we ran a new study about creating a library reading list (to replace our original Study 2A). We first conducted a pretest with an independent sample (N=550) where participants rated the importance of 21 different author attributes when creating a library reading list on a scale from 1 (Not at all important) to 7 (Very important). (Supplementary Study S3 in the new manuscript.) This pre-test revealed that people consider a number of author attributes to be similarly important (or more important in some cases) than an author’s gender (M_importance = 3.80) when generating a reading list. Specifically, participants reported the following attributes to be similarly important: whether authors wrote works spanning multiple genres (M=4.32, p=.265), whether authors had at least one book with 30 million or more copies sold (M=4.00, p=.679), and whether authors had works in continuous print for over 50 years (M=4.20, p=.363). Further, one-sided equivalence tests confirmed that these comparison attributes are not rated as significantly less important than gender (all ps < .05). Our new study (Study 4B) compares feedback on these pre-tested attributes of similar importance to feedback on gender. In this new study, we find that giving people feedback about the gender of the authors they've selected when selecting authors for a library reading list increases the likelihood of selecting a female author by 11.9 percentage points (p < .001), whereas giving people feedback about these other pre-tested attributes of similar importance produces virtually no change in selection behavior (spanning multiple genres: -8.2 pp, p = .057; 50+ years in print: 2.1 pp, p = .581; 30M+ copies sold: 1.2 pp, p = .764).
Thinking about the task abstractly, a participant gets a list of potential options (i=1,...,n) that have, say, j different  characteristics, x_{ij}.  A broad set of research investigates how people make these kind of choices, or put differently, how they weigh the characteristics or cues.  What makes this different from a number of selection tasks is that it is possibly a portfolio problem, so the weights may potentially depend on what other options have been chosen.  So, feedback could inform two very different decision inputs -- whether I have appropriately weighted the characteristics, or whether I have balanced the options in the chosen portfolio.  It is not clear to be which of these the authors believe is relevant.  The authors need to make clear what they think is going on here. RESPONSE: Thank you for pressing us to clarify whether we believe our effects reflect a portfolio problem versus an attribute weighting problem. We have revised the manuscript in an attempt to make this distinction explicit.
Specifically, we now state in our introduction (see p. 7) that our studies focus on how evaluators respond to receiving feedback about their initial portfolio of six (or seven) selections. We clarify in our theorizing that feedback makes the overall portfolio’s balance more salient than it would be in the absence of feedback.  Further, we clarify that all of our studies involve sequential choices: in all conditions, participants first choose a portfolio of selections (or in Study 1 learn about a portfolio of prior selections), then receive feedback, then make an additional choice about what to add to their portfolio. Feedback occurs after selections are made by necessity (i.e., you cannot receive feedback on decisions you have not yet made). For this reason, we do not theorize about whether the sequential nature of the selection is a relevant feature because it is a feature we hold constant and that it is necessarily held constant anytime feedback is involved in this context. 
We now discuss prior research on how the way a choice is represented can shape the diversity of selections (citing Simonson, 1990, Bohnet & Bazerman, 2016, and Chang et al., 2020) as a related literature given that we too are studying how a change in the circumstances under which choices are made shifts the diversity of subsequent selections.  However, we hold sequential vs. simultaneous choice constant (differentiating ourselves from Simonson, 1990), we hold joint vs. separate evaluation constant (differentiating ourselves from Bohnet & Bazerman, 2016) and we hold isolated versus group choices constant (differentiating ourselves from Chang et al., 2020). In short, while prior studies have also experimentally adjusted how options are presented and shown that this can affect the diversity of the selections people subsequently make, our work examines how people respond when descriptive feedback about a portfolio of past choices is provided, which is a distinct research question from the questions that have been studied in prior scholarship.  Our revised manuscript seeks to make this distinction clearer in both the Introduction (see pp. 7-8) and General Discussion (see p. 41). We hope these edits have sufficiently addressed your comment, and we welcome any additional feedback you may have.

RESPONSE: We now explicitly theorize that correction occurs when feedback about the race or gender of past selectees reveals that a person’s selections could be perceived as violating implicit injunctive norms about appropriate representation of women or racial minorities. These normative rates of selection of women or racial minorities may be different from the base rate of women or racial minorities in the selection pool – and our data suggest that evaluators’ reference point for “appropriate” representation is substantially higher than the base rate in the candidate pools for many of our studies. For example, in Studies 3A and 3B (previously Studies 1A and 1B), participants initially selected women and racial minorities at approximately the base rate (around 16-20%), yet feedback still produced large treatment effects, with participants increasing their selection of women and racial minorities by 15-20 percentage points. This suggests that selections matching low base rates are perceived as falling short of what would be considered appropriate, triggering concerns about appearing discriminatory.

RESPONSE: We now more clearly theorize that this correction is driven by motivation to respond without prejudice, which is the desire to appear fair and egalitarian and to avoid being perceived as anti-women or anti-minority (these are really the same thing in this context). We clarify that motivation to respond without prejudice stems from both external reputational concerns (e.g., the need to appear fair, egalitarian, or not anti-women) to others, as well as internal self-image concerns (maintaining a view of oneself as unbiased). We theorize that both internal and external motivations to respond without prejudice drive the need to correct in the face of feedback, and we provide evidence consistent with this through mediation in Studies 4A and 4B.

Furthermore, we address the possibility that feedback simply identifies a deficiency in the decision process. If this were the primary driver, we would expect participants to correct their selections similarly regardless of the attribute highlighted. However, we consistently find that feedback about gender and race leads to significantly larger selections of women and racial minorities than feedback about other attributes increases selections of those attributes. This holds true even when those other attributes are rated as equally important, as shown in Study 4B. This pattern suggests that the effect is not merely about fixing a general process flaw but is specifically driven by the normative concerns associated with the underrepresentation of women or racial minorities.
Third, and pointed out by the last point, why are the first six choices something that participants feel they need to correct? RESPONSE: We have sought to more clearly distinguish our work from prior research on variety-seeking and choice presentation format. Our theory is not about the initial selections themselves; they do not inherently “need” correction, and we are not positing that participants are failing to seek variety or making poor decisions. Rather, we propose that the descriptive feedback intervention we test makes the demographic composition of their portfolio of initial selections salient in a way that triggers concerns about potential norm violations. If indeed people find that their initial selections lacked diversity (as is the case in many of our studies by design), only then do we theorize that they will make a correction. We now clarify in our Introduction (pp. 7-8) and General Discussion (p. 39) that all of our studies hold constant the sequential nature of selection (both treatment and control groups make sequential choices), which differentiates our work from the work of Simonson (1990) on variety seeking. We also hold constant joint versus separate evaluation (all participants make joint evaluations, differentiating our work from Bohnet et al., 2016) and isolated versus group choices (all participants make group choices, differentiating our work from Chang et al., 2020). While these prior studies have shown that how options are presented affects the diversity of selections, our contribution is demonstrating that descriptive feedback about a prior portfolio of selections specifically triggers reputational concerns that can motivate changes in future decisions.

If participants were simply using the pool base rate as their reference point, we would expect feedback to have no effect on those who had already selected women or racial minorities at or above this threshold. Instead, as Table R1 shows, we find that the effect of feedback remains consistently directionally positive and often remains significant (despite the reduced statistical power) even just among participants who selected women or racial minorities at rates exceeding the base rate. For example, in Study 3A, 75% (N=753) of participants selected racial minorities at or above the 13.3% base rate, and the effect of descriptive feedback about the racial composition of past selectees in this subpopulation on subsequent selections of racial minorities remained positive and significant (8.89 pp, p = .002). These findings suggest that participants might be comparing their selections to an internal normative benchmark that exceeds the base rate at which women and minorities were present in our candidate pools, perhaps striving for something closer to population parity (as you suggested) for representation to be perceived as “fair” representation.
However, as our Study 5 demonstrates, when the base rate of women in the candidate pool is high enough (75%) that women are chosen at an average rate of approximately 61%, descriptive feedback’s impact reverses direction and reduces the likelihood participants subsequently select more women. While the rate of selecting women (or URMs) in our studies is endogenous, we do present data (see Table 4 in the manuscript) showing that the effect of feedback is moderated by the proportion of women initially selected in some studies. Specifically, as this proportion increases, the positive effect of descriptive feedback on the likelihood of subsequently selecting a member of a historically disadvantaged group decreases and eventually reverses in direction. Importantly, our mediation analyses in Study 5 suggest that this pattern reflects a shift in the underlying mechanism that shifts subsequent decisions after people receive descriptive feedback about past choices. When women are underrepresented, feedback triggers a motivation to respond without prejudice, producing a large increase in the likelihood a participant subsequently selects a woman. When women are overrepresented, however, feedback instead primarily triggers fairness concerns, which produce a smaller (though still significant) effect in the opposite direction, increasing the likelihood participants subsequently select a man. This suggests that concerns about prejudice are a more potent motivator of behavior change than general fairness concerns, though both can change behavior in this context. This also helps explain why the effect of feedback attenuates and reverses as the proportion of women (or racial minorities) initially selected increases.

We viewed the contribution of our paper as establishing the efficacy of feedback interventions in settings where women and racial minorities are underrepresented and examining the mechanism responsible for this pattern.

To directly address your question about whether making other attributes “salient and important” would produce effects comparable to providing feedback about the gender or racial composition of prior selectees, we feel it would be helpful to clarify what we see as our paper’s aims and contribution. In many studies examining the effects of descriptive feedback, that feedback is coupled with an explicit descriptive norm, prescription, or expectation around the goal or standard a person should aim to achieve with feedback in hand (e.g., Bandura and Cervone, 1983; Tiefenbeck et al., 2018; Schultz et al., 2007); for example, in Tiefenbeck et al. (2018), real-time feedback on shower energy consumption is provided alongside an energy efficiency rating from A to G and a shrinking ice floe animation that explicitly signals when consumption exceeds desirable levels. And it has been theorized that descriptive feedback’s impact is largely contingent on the coupling of this feedback with a clear benchmark (Kluger & DeNisi, 1996, 1998). It is worth noting that we also see fairness concerns significantly mediate the effect of feedback on the increased selection of women when women are underrepresented (Indirect Effect = 0.101, 95% CI = [0.067, 0.137], p < .001), likely because of the high correlation between this construct and motivation to respond without prejudice (see Table R4). 
Taken together, these findings align with your intuition that people are unlikely to feel they “seem prejudiced” for selecting too many women; however, our participants do appear to worry that they have been unfair to men in a context where they have been sensitized to worry about representation, and this leads to a smaller but still significant effect of feedback on their subsequent choices.  Broadly, this finding is consistent with our theorizing on the effects of implicit social norms and feedback on decision making and suggests interesting directions for future research into other settings where similar fairness concerns may affect how people respond to feedback. It also suggests that feedback can be helpful not only in settings where women and minorities have been historically underrepresented (e.g., entrepreneurship, STEM) but also may helpful for restoring balance in settings where they have been overrepresented (e.g., nursing, childcare), though perhaps to a lesser degree.

RESPONSE: Thank you for returning to this important question about the theoretical scope of our effects. We have revised our General Discussion (see p. 44) to address your point about whether there is something unique about race and gender as moralized issues. Specifically, we now explicitly discuss that these attributes are distinctive because they are often immediately visible, governed by strong normative prohibitions backed by legal frameworks, and carry clear consequences for both reputation and self-image.
