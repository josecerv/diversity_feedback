RESPONSE: Thank you for raising this alternative. We agree it is worth testing directly, and we have run a follow-up analysis (presented in Table R1 below) to evaluate it. We now report these analyses in our revised manuscript in [WHERE?]
To operationalize “appropriate use,” we use each attribute’s base rate in the candidate pool. In each study, participants choose from a fixed pool with a known proportion of candidates having a given attribute (for example, 32% of the authors in Study 4A are racial minorities). The base rate is a neutral benchmark for proportional selection. Arguably, a participant whose initial selection rate is below the base rate has under-weighted the attribute, leaving feedback room to increase that attribute’s selection. Conversely, if a participant selects at or above the base rate, then there is no proportional gap left for feedback to close. For each of the five studies in which participants generated their own initial portfolios (Studies 2, 3A, 3B, 4A, 4B), we split the sample into Below-Pool and At/Above-Pool subgroups by attribute and re-estimated the feedback effect within each subgroup.
As shown in Table R1, we show that even when participants’ initial selections are below the base rate, there is no subsequent increase in that attribute’s selections. More specifically, across the fifteen Below-Pool comparison-attribute coefficients, thirteen are statistically null, one is marginally positive (+9.53 percentage points, p = .07), and one is marginally negative (-16.47 percentage points, p = .07). A random-effects mini meta-analysis (Goh et al., 2016) pooling the fifteen estimates yields +0.82 percentage points (95% CI [-2.48, 4.13], z = 0.49, p = .63): comparison feedback does not move participants even when we would expect the largest effect of feedback.
That said, we acknowledge that “appropriate use” can also be read more broadly as preference-consistent selection (e.g., under-selecting long-duration films because participants dislike them), which the present subgroup test cannot rule out.

Although several large firms have retreated from explicit DEI rhetoric since 2024, much of this retreat reflects a rebranding of existing functions rather than an abandonment of the underlying commitments. For example, Constellation Brands renamed its Diversity and Inclusion function as the Inclusive Culture Team; Kohl’s renamed its Chief DEI Officer as the Chief Inclusion and Belonging Officer; and Nationwide, UPS, Amazon, and McDonald’s have similarly migrated to language emphasizing “belonging,” “respect,” “fairness,” or “inclusive experiences” (CNBC, 2025; HRGrapevine, 2025; Marketplace, 2026). Consistent with this rebranding pattern, Paradigm reports a 22% decrease in Fortune 100 references to “DEI/diversity” between 2023 and 2024 alongside a 59% increase in references to “belonging” (Paradigm, 2025). Investor sentiment has also remained largely supportive of these commitments: Costco shareholders rejected an anti-DEI proposal by 98%, and similar proposals at Apple, Levi’s, John Deere, and Goldman Sachs failed (CBS, 2025; Marketplace, 2025). Australia’s Workplace Gender Equality Amendment (Closing the Gender Pay Gap) Act 2023 mandates public reporting of firm-level gender representation and pay-gap information (Ressia, 2024). Together, these developments reflect a continued and broadening global demand for descriptive information about demographic representation, even as U.S. rhetoric continues to evolve.
Taken together, we argue that while public-facing DEI rhetoric has receded in the U.S., organizational commitments to diversity remain substantively intact, rebranded rather than abandoned and reinforced by sustained investor support, majority public backing, and expanding international regulatory accountability. RESPONSE: Thank you for this thoughtful comment. We have sharpened the theoretical contribution in the revised General Discussion (pp. X), building on the scope-condition discussion already present in the Future Directions and Limitations section.
In particular, we now distinguish two pathways through which descriptive feedback can change selection decisions, both of which the prior draft already touched on but had not explicitly separated. The external pathway is broader than any one firm’s stated DEI climate. Selections are observable by peers, candidates, supervisors, and downstream audiences, and the implicit norm against being seen as prejudiced is sustained by anti-discrimination law, civil-society pressure, employee and customer scrutiny, and broader reputational concerns. From this perspective, organizations’ explicit DEI commitments are themselves often responses to external pressures (Levi & Fried, 2024); a firm’s retreat from DEI rhetoric reflects shifting external pressure on the firm, not a collapse of the broader social pressure on individual selection decisions. The internal pathway, by contrast, refers to selectors’ personally held egalitarian beliefs and self-image as non-prejudiced decision makers. This pathway does not vary with organizational climate, and it is the pathway most directly supported by Study 4, in which feedback was delivered privately and internal motivation to respond without prejudice mediated subsequent selections.
We did test whether political ideology moderated our effect in Study 4 and observed a null interaction. While we are cautious about over-interpreting null results, the pattern is suggestive that the effect operates across partisan lines, leaving room for the external and internal motivations described above to be at work.
As we already note in the General Discussion, we view motivation to respond without prejudice as one mechanism we isolate, not the only one descriptive feedback can plausibly activate. Fairness norms and broader concerns about appropriate representation may also be triggered (see also Study 5’s mechanism analysis). We agree that a direct empirical manipulation of motivation to respond without prejudice would be a particularly clean future test, and we now flag this as a future-research direction.
