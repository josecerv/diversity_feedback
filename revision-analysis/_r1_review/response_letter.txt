RESPONSE: Thank you for raising this alternative. We agree it is worth testing directly, and we report a follow-up analysis below (Table R1). We have also added a footnote to the Discussion of Study 4B (p. X) that summarizes this evidence and makes the underlying theoretical claim more explicit in the manuscript.
In our studies we make no assumptions about the normative or “appropriate” level for any attribute in the absence of explicit goals or prescriptions; we anticipate that the activation of a reputational or image concern occurs only after participants receive descriptive feedback, and only for race and gender. This is consistent with the theoretical framing already in our paper: descriptive feedback shifts behavior on a given dimension only when that dimension carries an implicit injunctive norm violation of which descriptive feedback calls into question, which we have argued holds for race and gender but not for the comparison attributes we test (see Introduction, p. X, and General Discussion, p. X).
That said, we can examine directly what happens when selections include zero of a given attribute (e.g., 0% are political leaders, 0% are women). Among participants whose initial selections contained zero of an attribute, the inference that the attribute is “missing” from the portfolio may produce some directional motivation to select one of those candidates next, in order to maintain appropriate levels. independent of any injunctive norm; if our race/gender feedback effects were driven by this inference rather than by an underlying norm, we would expect comparable feedback effects on the comparison attributes among the subset of participants who picked zero of them initially. We do not observe that. Pooling participants across Studies 2, 3A, 3B, 4A, and 4B in a study-by-attribute fixed-effects regression with participant-clustered standard errors, restricted to the participant-attribute observations in which the initial selection contained zero of the focal attribute, race/gender feedback increases the probability of selecting a candidate with the focal attribute by +21.28 percentage points (p < .0001, N = 1,222 across all five studies), while comparison-attribute feedback increases that probability by only +6.83 percentage points, which is not statistically distinguishable from zero (p = .21, N = 429 across six attribute-by-study cells; the remaining nine cells are omitted because the comparison attribute is common in its candidate pool and almost no participant picked zero of it initially). A Wald test of the difference between the two pooled coefficients yields +14.46 percentage points (p = .016, 95% CI [+2.70, +26.21]).

Table R1 reports each per-cell estimate together with the pooled coefficients and the Wald-test difference. The first column lists each study and its total sample size; the second column lists the focal attribute, the race or gender attribute for the study followed by each estimable comparison attribute; and the third column reports the feedback effect for that attribute, restricted to participants whose initial selection contained zero of it. Although several large firms have retreated from explicit DEI rhetoric since 2024, much of this retreat reflects a rebranding of existing functions rather than an abandonment of the underlying commitments. For example, Constellation Brands renamed its Diversity and Inclusion function as the Inclusive Culture Team; Kohl’s renamed its Chief DEI Officer as the Chief Inclusion and Belonging Officer; and Nationwide, UPS, Amazon, and McDonald’s have similarly migrated to language emphasizing “belonging,” “respect,” “fairness,” or “inclusive experiences” (CNBC, 2025; HRGrapevine, 2025; Marketplace, 2026). RESPONSE: Thank you for this thoughtful comment. We have sharpened the theoretical contribution in the revised General Discussion (pp. X), building on the scope-condition discussion already present in the Future Directions and Limitations section.
In particular, we now distinguish two pathways through which descriptive feedback can change selection decisions, both of which the prior draft already touched on but had not explicitly separated. The external pathway is broader than any one firm’s stated DEI climate. Selections are observable by peers, candidates, supervisors, and downstream audiences, and the implicit norm against being seen as prejudiced is sustained by anti-discrimination law, civil-society pressure, employee and customer scrutiny, and broader reputational concerns. From this perspective, organizations’ explicit DEI commitments are themselves often responses to external pressures (Levi & Fried, 2024); a firm’s retreat from DEI rhetoric reflects shifting external pressure on the firm, not a collapse of the broader social pressure on individual selection decisions. The internal pathway, by contrast, refers to selectors’ personally held egalitarian beliefs and self-image as non-prejudiced decision makers. This pathway does not vary with organizational climate, and it is the pathway most directly supported by Study 4, in which feedback was delivered privately and internal motivation to respond without prejudice mediated subsequent selections.
We did test whether political ideology moderated our effect in Study 4 and observed a null interaction. While we are cautious about over-interpreting null results, the pattern is suggestive that the effect operates across partisan lines, leaving room for the external and internal motivations described above to be at work.
As we already note in the General Discussion, we view motivation to respond without prejudice as one mechanism we isolate, not the only one descriptive feedback can plausibly activate. Fairness norms and broader concerns about appropriate representation may also be triggered (see also Study 5’s mechanism analysis). We agree that a direct empirical manipulation of motivation to respond without prejudice would be a particularly clean future test, and we now flag this as a future-research direction.
