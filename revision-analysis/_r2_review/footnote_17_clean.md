One alternative explanation is that feedback about other attributes shows smaller effects simply because participants are already using those attributes appropriately in their initial selections, leaving little room for feedback to move them further. To test this, we pooled data from Studies 2, 3A, 3B, and 4B and looked only at participants whose initial selections included zero films or books with a given attribute (e.g., a participant in Study 3A whose seven initial films included no films released after 2010, or a participant in Study 4B whose six initial books included no women authors). We exclude Study 4A from this analysis because no participants in that study made initial selections containing zero books with an attribute we targeted with feedback besides the author’s race. Learning you selected zero films or books with an attribute is a signal of underrepresentation on that dimension, and if feedback’s effect simply reflects a reaction to underrepresentation, it should move participants regardless of which attribute is involved. Even within the subset of our participant-feedback pairs in which participants learned they had selected no films or books with a given attribute, however, feedback that initial selections included no women or racial minorities increased the likelihood of subsequently selecting a woman or minority, respectively, significantly (by 22.50 percentage points on average; women: +27.59 pp; racial minorities: +9.77 pp; p < .001, N = 864), whereas feedback that initial selections included no books or films with any other attribute (e.g., a big budget, a recent release year) did not significantly shift subsequent selections of books or films with that attribute (other descriptive feedback: +7.25; p = .19, N = 414). A Wald test confirms the gap in feedback’s impact is significant (p = .016, 95% CI [+2.87, +27.63]). See Appendix Section B for more details on this analysis.
