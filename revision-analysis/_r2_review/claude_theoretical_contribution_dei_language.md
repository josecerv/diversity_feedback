## Current paragraph 171 (verbatim, target for revision)

> Our work makes two primary theoretical contributions. First, we offer new insights about how and when descriptive feedback motivates behavior change. According to Feedback Intervention Theory, feedback is most effective when paired with explicit goals, standards, or explicit injunctive norms that signal a clear gap between current performance and a desired state (Kluger & DeNisi, 1996; Locke & Latham, 2019; Schultz et al., 2007; Schultz et al., 2018). Purely descriptive feedback is often viewed as insufficient to drive change because it lacks a directive. We show that descriptive feedback can consistently motivate behavior change in the absence of explicit prescriptions. When such feedback targets DEI-relevant attributes of past selections and suggests women or minorities have been overlooked, the feedback itself elicits reputational or self-image concerns – specifically, internal and external motivation to respond without prejudice. External motivation to respond without prejudice operates when selection decisions are observable to colleagues, candidates, supervisors, customers, employees, or stakeholders, and is sustained primarily by anti-discrimination law and broader reputational pressures. When U.S. firms pull back from their public DEI rhetoric, such rhetoric is best understood as one downstream signal of these broader pressures rather than as their source (Levi & Fried, 2024), so the external pathway should remain operative even as organizational DEI commitments ebb and flow. Internal motivation to respond without prejudice, by contrast, reflects evaluators' own egalitarian commitments and desire to view themselves as non-prejudiced decision makers. Because internal motivation comes from a person's own values rather than from signals their organization sends, this pathway should be relatively stable even as an organization's DEI climate shifts. Study 4 most directly supports this pathway: there, feedback was delivered privately, and internal motivation emerged as the primary driver of subsequent selections. In the context of diversity, an explicit goal, target, social norm or injunctive norm does not need to be coupled with feedback to induce a response because an implicit injunctive norm to avoid prejudice has already been internalized by the decision-maker. This suggests that the utility of providing descriptive feedback absent an explicit goal, target or norm lies in its ability to focus attention on past behavior in arenas where individuals already harbor self-image or reputational concerns.

## Recommended proposal (two paragraphs)

### New paragraph 171

> Our work makes two primary theoretical contributions. First, we offer new insights about how and when descriptive feedback motivates behavior change. According to Feedback Intervention Theory, feedback is most effective when paired with explicit goals, standards, or explicit injunctive norms that signal a clear gap between current performance and a desired state (Kluger & DeNisi, 1996; Locke & Latham, 2019; Schultz et al., 2007; Schultz et al., 2018). Purely descriptive feedback is often viewed as insufficient to drive change because it lacks a directive. We show that descriptive feedback can consistently motivate behavior change in the absence of explicit prescriptions. When such feedback targets DEI-relevant attributes of past selections and suggests women or minorities have been overlooked, the feedback itself activates two distinct motivations to respond without prejudice (Plant & Devine, 1998): an internal motivation, rooted in a decision maker's own egalitarian commitments and desire to view themselves as non-prejudiced, and an external motivation, rooted in concern about how their selections will be judged by others. In the context of diversity, an explicit goal, target, social norm, or injunctive norm does not need to be coupled with feedback to induce a response because an implicit injunctive norm to avoid prejudice has already been internalized by the decision maker. This suggests that the utility of providing descriptive feedback absent an explicit goal, target, or norm lies in its ability to focus attention on past behavior in arenas where individuals already harbor self-image or reputational concerns.

### New paragraph 171b

> In recent years, the rhetoric organizations use around DEI has ebbed and flowed, expanding in some periods and contracting in others. We argue, however, that the intervention's effect is not contingent on these shifts, because both pathways that drive it sit largely outside any one organization's stated commitments. The external pathway is broader than firm-level climate: a decision maker's selections are observed by coworkers, direct reports, candidates, and other professional peers, and these observers continue to form opinions about whether the selections are fair regardless of how a firm publicly describes its DEI commitments. Anti-discrimination law and everyday workplace norms also continue to apply (Levi & Fried, 2024). The internal pathway is even less sensitive to organizational rhetoric. It reflects a decision maker's own commitment to fairness and desire to view themselves as non-prejudiced (Plant & Devine, 1998), and Study 4 most directly supports this pathway, where feedback was delivered privately and internal motivation emerged as the primary driver of subsequent selections.
