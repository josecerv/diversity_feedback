# Converged rewrite v2: Section 3 opener

## Existing paragraph (verbatim from manuscript_gdoc_text.txt line 21)

> Globally,  many companies are making sustained investments to increase the representation of women and minorities in their ranks (Kirkland & Bohnet, 2017; Society for Human Resource Management [SHRM], 2022) and are eager for new tools to support their diversification goals given the potential benefits of harnessing all types of talent (Richard, 2000; Hsieh et al., 2019; cf. Jehn et al., 1999). Providing regular feedback to employees about the demographics of whom they have previously chosen to spotlight, hire and promote is an approach some organizations (such as NPR, Salesforce, and the BBC) are adopting in the hopes that such feedback will spur decision makers to select more diverse candidates, but it is an open question whether purely descriptive feedback can be an effective intervention.

## Proposed replacement (recommended)

> Increasing the representation of women and racial minorities in workplaces, leadership, and other high-visibility roles remains a salient concern for organizations and the publics they serve, even as the corporate rhetoric around DEI has expanded and contracted over time (Devine & Ash, 2022; Levi & Fried, 2024). Some organizations have responded by giving decision makers regular feedback on the demographics of those they have previously chosen to spotlight, hire, or promote (e.g., NPR, Salesforce, the BBC), in the hope that such feedback will spur more inclusive subsequent selections. Whether purely descriptive feedback of this kind can be an effective intervention remains an open empirical question.

## Why this version

- **Acknowledges the tides, in one clause.** "Even as the corporate rhetoric around DEI has expanded and contracted over time" — no years, no firms, no pullback / surge framing, no implied valence about which direction is current.
- **Proclaims importance, in one clause.** "Remains a salient concern for organizations and the publics they serve" — asserts that the question matters now, without tethering that to either "even more important now because firms are pulling back" or "important now because firms are investing." It would read the same in 2018 or 2026.
- **Stays out of paragraph 2's lane.** No mention of mechanism (reputational concerns, self-image, motivation to respond without prejudice). Paragraph 2 introduces those.
- **Stays out of paragraph 3's lane.** No anti-discrimination law, no international transparency directives, no "individuals' own egalitarian commitments." Paragraph 3 marshals those.
- **Preserves the original arc.** Three beats: (a) the question matters, (b) some organizations have tried descriptive feedback (NPR / Salesforce / BBC), (c) whether it actually works is an open empirical question.
- **Modernizes citations.** Out: Kirkland & Bohnet (2017), SHRM (2022), Richard (2000), Hsieh et al. (2019), Jehn et al. (1999). In: Devine & Ash (2022) for the enduring scholarly and organizational interest in diversity programs; Levi & Fried (2024) for the rhetoric fluctuation. Both are already in the R2 reference list.

## What I rejected from Codex's v2

- **"Selection patterns that descriptive feedback makes visible" / "by making the demographic composition of prior decisions salient."** Codex slipped into describing how the intervention works mechanistically. Paragraph 2 of the section opens by introducing that mechanism; the opener should not pre-empt it.
- **"Managers still routinely make choices…"** This is a description, not an assertion of importance. It softens the relevance claim that JC asked for.
- **"Adopting… without requiring an explicit diversity goal."** Foreshadows the FIT-and-implicit-norm theoretical distinction that paragraph 2 frames more carefully.
