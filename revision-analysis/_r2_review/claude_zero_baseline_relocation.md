# Claude Recommendation: Zero-Baseline Relocation (round 2)

## What I missed in round 1

Round 1 framed the zero-baseline analysis as a sibling to the Study 5 mini-meta because both are cross-study re-analyses about "baseline." That framing was wrong. The mini-meta tests *continuous proportion × feedback* as the rationale for Study 5's experimental manipulation of baseline. The zero-baseline analysis answers a different question: whether the race/gender vs. comparison-attribute differential is real, or whether comparison attributes are just used appropriately at baseline. Two questions, two narratives. Forcing them to share a paragraph creates exactly the jarring effect JC felt.

## The DE's R1 critique, re-read

Two prongs:
1. **Importance**: comparison attributes might be less important than race/gender (so naturally less responsive to feedback).
2. **Appropriate initial use**: people might already use comparison attributes appropriately in initial selections (so no room for feedback to shift).

The manuscript already engages prong 1 in two places:
- Study 1 Methods: the new N=300 NPR importance pretest (recently inserted).
- Study 4B preamble (line 115): the existing N=550 author-attribute importance pretest (Appendix Section S4).

The manuscript does NOT currently engage prong 2 anywhere. That is the gap the zero-baseline analysis fills.

## The most important sentence in this whole analysis

**Manuscript line 99 (Study 4 preamble, second sentence)** explicitly states the comparison-attribute robustness concern:

> "We also attempted to rule out the possibility that the 'comparison' attributes summarized in descriptive feedback (alongside feedback about the race or gender of past selectees) in our prior studies may have been perceived as less important to participants than the race and gender of candidates. In Study 4B, we selected comparison attributes after pre-testing them for importance, and we ensured that all were rated at least as important as gender or race. See Appendix Study S4 for a complete description of our pre-testing procedure and results."

This paragraph engages prong 1 (importance). Adding prong 2 (appropriate use) here gives Study 4 a coherent rule-out narrative: *two siblings, both ruled out.* This is where the manuscript already invites the reader into the "is the differential effect real?" question.

## Exact prose

### Main-text extension to Study 4 preamble (append to the existing paragraph at line 99)

> We further considered the possibility, raised by reviewers, that participants may have already been using the comparison attributes appropriately in their initial selections, leaving little room for feedback about those attributes to shift behavior. To address this, we examined the subset of participants whose initial selections contained zero candidates with the focal attribute, where appropriate prior use cannot account for non-response to feedback. Pooling participants across Studies 2, 3A, 3B, and 4B (Study 4A is excluded because no participant began with zero of any comparison attribute), the differential effect held: race or gender feedback raised the probability of selecting a racial minority or woman by 22.50 percentage points (*p* < .001, *N* = 864), whereas comparison-attribute feedback shifted subsequent selections by only +7.25 percentage points (*p* = .19, *N* = 414), a difference confirmed by a Wald test (*p* = .016). Full results are reported in Appendix Section S[X].

This integrates the appropriate-use rule-out alongside the importance rule-out, in JC's voice (no em dashes; italic *p* and *N*; curly quotes auto-applied by Gdocs; uses "we further considered," "we examined," "the differential effect held" — JC's existing patterns from the response letter).
