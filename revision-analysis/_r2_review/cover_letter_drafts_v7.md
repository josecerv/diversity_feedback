## A. Cover-letter summary — v7 (4 numbered bullets)

> Here is a summary of the most notable changes we have made in this revision:
>
> **1.** We have addressed your two empirical requests.
>
> First, the new Study 1 importance post‑test (N = 300) confirms that the comparison attributes were not rated less important than gender, so importance differences cannot explain why only gender feedback moved selections.
>
> Second, the new pooled analysis (Table R1), restricted to participants whose initial selections contained zero of the focal attribute, shows that race and gender feedback significantly raises subsequent selection of women and racial minorities, whereas comparison‑attribute feedback does not. Both findings support our claim that descriptive feedback shifts behavior on race and gender because those dimensions carry an implicit injunctive norm against prejudiced selection, not because they happen to be under‑selected to begin with.
>
> **2.** We have streamlined the empirical sections of the paper following your guidance on length and repetition. The revised manuscript is now [X] pages and reflects a substantial streamlining of the Methods and Results sections, particularly for later studies to avoid unnecessary repetition and redundancy.
>
> **3.** In response to Reviewer 2, we have updated the Introduction's discussion of the current landscape around DEI and used that update to sharpen our theoretical claims. We openly acknowledge that U.S. appetite for new DEI initiatives has retreated since 2024, while noting three parallel patterns that keep our central question relevant: international transparency requirements continue to expand, U.S. public and stakeholder support for corporate DEI remains broadly intact, and many firms have rebranded their diversity work rather than dismantling it. These patterns help clarify why the motivation to respond without prejudice that drives our effect operates through two pathways: an external pathway, sustained by anti‑discrimination law, the visibility of selection decisions, and broader public expectations, and an internal pathway, rooted in evaluators' own egalitarian commitments and self‑image. We therefore expect the effect to be strongest where selections are visible and evaluators care about being seen as fair, and weakest in fully private settings or in organizations that openly reject egalitarian norms. The General Discussion now flags organizational DEI climate as a scope condition for future research.
>
> **4.** In response to Reviewer 1, we have modernized the references that anchor our theoretical framing in both the Introduction and General Discussion, drawing on more contemporary feedback, goal‑setting, and social‑norms work alongside the foundational citations we retain.

## Specific changes vs. v6

### Bullet 1 closing
- **v6**: "Full numbers and the caveats around using zero as a benchmark appear in our point-by-point response below."
- **v7**: "Both findings support our claim that descriptive feedback shifts behavior on race and gender because those dimensions carry an implicit injunctive norm against prejudiced selection, not because they happen to be under-selected to begin with."

The new closing does two things the old one didn't: (a) ties the two empirical results back to the paper's central theoretical claim, and (b) names the specific alternative we're ruling out (under-selection) rather than vaguely promising details below.

### Bullet 3 jargon reductions

| v6 phrasing | v7 phrasing |

| "framing of the current sociopolitical landscape" | "discussion of the current landscape" |
| "recent U.S. retreat from explicit DEI rhetoric" | "U.S. appetite for new DEI initiatives has retreated since 2024" |

The phrase **"motivation to respond without prejudice"** is preserved — it's the manuscript's named construct (Plant & Devine, 1998) and the rest of the letter and manuscript hinge on it. Same for "implicit injunctive norm" in bullet 1.

The external-pathway list (anti-discrimination law, visibility, public expectations) is now slightly more concrete than codex's super-compact "legal, reputational, and normative pressures." That re-spending of words is intentional: the concrete list shows the reader what those pressures actually are, which makes the abstract claim more credible.
