RESPONSE: Thank you for raising this possibility. We now both discuss it (see p. X) and a follow-up analysis that attempts to address this point. 
We expect that a reputational or image concern is activated only after participants receive descriptive feedback suggesting that women and minorities have been vastly underrepresented in their past selections compared to the U.S. population. If underrepresentation of any group is responsible for our effects, then if the descriptive feedback we provided on some other selection attribute (say, the age of prior selectees) suggested a massive underrepresentation (say, of people over 50), this might drive a similar reaction. We conducted an additional analysis to try to address this possibility. Assuming that selecting zero options with a given attribute would always signal underrepresentation, we test the hypothesis that feedback shifts in who is subsequently selected to make up for underrepresentation not just when it comes to race and gender but on any dimension. To examine this hypothesis, we pooled data from Studies 2, 3A, 3B, and 4B (clustering standard errors by participant). We only retain observations involving participants who made an initial set of selections that included zero books or movies with a given attribute (e.g., a set of films in Study 3A that included zero films released after 2010, or a set of books in Study 4B that included zero books with women authors). We find that among participants who selected zero women or racial minorities initially, across these studies, receiving this feedback (that they had selected no women or racial minorities) significantly increases the probability of selecting a racial minority or woman as their final selection, respectively, by 22.50 percentage points (p < .001, N = 864). Among participants who initially selected zero movies, books, or authors with any of the other attributes under study (e.g., budget, year of release, or protagonist’s profession), feedback informing them that none of their selectees had those other attributes did not significantly alter their probability of subsequently selecting an additional movie, book, or author with the relevant attribute (b = 7.25 percentage points; p = .19, N = 414). A Wald test of the difference between the two pooled coefficients confirms that the pooled effect of feedback on subsequent choices is significantly larger when feedback is about the race or gender of initial selections than when it is about other features of initial selections (p = .016, 95% CI [+2.87, +27.63]). We have added a footnote to the General Discussion (p. X), anchored on the sentence where we first state the differential effect of race or gender feedback versus comparison-attribute feedback. For instance a 2025 Bentley/Gallup survey reports that 69% of U.S. adults believe businesses should promote DEI (Bentley/Gallup, 2025), and anti-DEI shareholder proposals have failed at numerous prominent organizations, including Costco, Apple, Levi’s, John Deere, Goldman Sachs, and others (CBS, 2025; Marketplace, 2025). Instead, it is to show that descriptive feedback about past selection decisions can change subsequent decisions when the patterns highlighted raise concerns about appearing prejudiced or failing to live up to your own egalitarian standards. To the extent that corporate messaging and formal diversity commitments are one source of people’s motivation to respond without prejudice, their retreat may weaken the effects we document. However, such organizational commitments are only one potential reason why people prefer not to behave in ways that could be seen as prejudiced. Anti-discrimination law, international transparency requirements, employee and stakeholder expectations, and individuals’ own egalitarian commitments continue to make race and gender consequential dimensions of evaluation, even when organizations no longer foreground DEI policies. We now explain that descriptive feedback should be most influential when past selection patterns raise active concerns about prejudice, and less influential in contexts where those concerns are absent, muted, or explicitly rejected. Practically, this also reinforces the relevance of studying descriptive feedback even in the current setting: in settings where organizations are reluctant to issue explicit diversity goals (as they might be in the U.S. at the moment), it arguably becomes more important to understand the effects of other steps companies might take, such as whether making past selection patterns visible can still shape who is subsequently selected, hired, promoted, platformed, or featured.

RESPONSE: Thank you for this thoughtful comment. As you note, we did test whether political ideology moderated our effect in Study 4 and overall observed no significant interaction. While we are cautious about over-interpreting null results, the pattern is suggestive that the effect operates across partisan lines, leaving room for the external and internal motivations to respond without prejudice described above to be at work. Consistent with this interpretation, another recent study by Kirgios et al. (2022) found in a field experiment that when seeking help from city counselors, women and racial minorities benefited from explicitly naming their demographic identity in an ask just as much when contacting Republicans and Democrats, and the underlying mechanism identified was a motivation to control prejudice - the same mechanism we identify as mediating our findings. Overall, the existing empirical evidence suggests political ideology does not seem to moderate motivation to control prejudice.
We share your view that there are already quite a lot of studies in this paper. Rather than adding another, we have sought to engage with the important issues you raise narratively (this also aligns with the DE’s guidance to prioritize streamlining the manuscript).  Specifically, we have sought to sharpen our discussion of our theoretical contribution in the revised General Discussion (pp. X), building on the scope-condition discussion already present in the Future Directions and Limitations section.
In particular, we now explicitly distinguish between two pathways through which descriptive feedback can change selection decisions. One pathway, the external motivation to control prejudice, should be driven both by a firm’s norms but also by the fact that selection decisions are often observable to colleagues, job candidates, supervisors, and other stakeholders. The implicit injunctive norm against prejudicial behavior is largely sustained by anti-discrimination laws, employee and customer scrutiny, and broader reputational concerns. The second pathway, internal motivation to control prejudice, by contrast, refers to selectors’ personally held egalitarian beliefs and self-image as non-prejudiced decision-makers. This second pathway should not vary with organizational climate, and it is the pathway most directly supported by Study 4 (a study in which feedback was delivered privately and internal motivation to respond without prejudice mediated subsequent selections).
Motivation to respond without prejudice is a key mechanism isolated in this paper, but it may not be the only mechanism descriptive feedback activates to change behavior. Fairness norms and broader concerns about appropriate representation may also drive the effects of feedback about past selections on future selection decisions (see also Study 5’s mechanism analysis, which found that fairness concerns drive people to select men when women are overrepresented in order to reduce gender imbalance). We now note that it would be valuable for future research to explore these additional plausible contributing mechanisms.
