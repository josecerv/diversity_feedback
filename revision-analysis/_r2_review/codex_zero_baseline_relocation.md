## PROSE DRAFT - REMOVAL

Delete the following zero-baseline material from the paragraph at line 133, beginning after the sentence ending with “Kirgios et al., 2022).” and before “Study 5”:

> Building on this, we conducted a pooled analysis across Studies 2, 3A, 3B, and 4B (excluding Study 4A, where no participant began with zero of any comparison attribute), restricting our sample to participants whose initial selections contained zero candidates with a given attribute. Selecting zero of an attribute is a clear signal of underrepresentation that should provoke a response to feedback regardless of which attribute is summarized. Race and gender feedback raised the probability of selecting a racial minority or woman, respectively, by 22.50 percentage points (*p* < .001, *N* = 864), whereas feedback about other attributes did not significantly shift subsequent selections among participants whose initial portfolios contained zero of that attribute (effect = +7.25 percentage points, *p* = .19, *N* = 414). A Wald test confirms the difference between the two pooled coefficients is significant (*p* = .016). Full results appear in Appendix Section S[X].

> Relatedly, we tested whether the smaller effects of comparison-attribute feedback could be explained by participants already using those attributes appropriately in their initial selections. We pooled data from Studies 2, 3A, 3B, and 4B, excluding Study 4A because no participant began with zero of any comparison attribute, and restricted the analysis to participants whose initial selections contained zero of a given attribute. If the race and gender effects simply reflected participants reacting to the absence of an attribute from their initial portfolios, comparison-attribute feedback should also shift later selections in this subsample. It did not. Race and gender feedback increased the probability of selecting a racial minority or woman by 22.50 percentage points (*p* < .001, *N* = 864), whereas feedback about comparison attributes increased the probability of selecting an option with the focal attribute by 7.25 percentage points, an effect that was not statistically significant (*p* = .19, *N* = 414). A Wald test confirmed that the pooled race and gender feedback effect was significantly larger than the pooled comparison-attribute feedback effect (*p* = .016; see Appendix Section S[X]).
