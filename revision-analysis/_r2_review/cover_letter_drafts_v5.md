## A. Cover-letter summary (5 numbered bullets)

> Here is a summary of the most notable changes we have made in this revision:
>
> **1.** We have addressed your two empirical requests. (i) A new Study 1 importance post‑test (N = 300) confirms that the comparison attributes were not rated less important than gender, so importance differences cannot explain why only gender feedback moved selections. (ii) A new pooled analysis (Table R1) restricted to participants whose initial selections contained zero of the focal attribute shows that race and gender feedback significantly raises subsequent selection of women and racial minorities, whereas comparison‑attribute feedback does not. Full numbers and the caveats around using zero as a benchmark appear in our point‑by‑point response below.
>
> **2.** We have streamlined the empirical sections of the paper following your guidance on length and repetition. The revised manuscript moves the regression specifications behind our secondary feedback comparisons into a dedicated Appendix section and trims redundant Methods and Results language across later studies.
>
> **3.** We have situated the paper in the current sociopolitical landscape around DEI in response to Reviewer 2. We openly acknowledge the recent U.S. retreat from explicit DEI rhetoric while noting three parallel patterns that keep our central question relevant: expanding international transparency requirements, sustained U.S. public and stakeholder support for corporate DEI, and within‑firm rebranding rather than dismantling of diversity work. Because the legal, reputational, and normative pressures that make race and gender salient operate well beyond any one firm's rhetoric, understanding when descriptive feedback shifts selection decisions remains practically important.
>
> **4.** In response to Reviewer 2's request for a more nuanced theoretical positioning, we have tightened the scope of our claims. We now distinguish two pathways: an external motivation to respond without prejudice sustained by anti‑discrimination law and the observability of selection decisions, and an internal motivation rooted in selectors' personally held egalitarian self‑image. The effect should generalize where selections are observable and selectors hold some egalitarian commitments, and attenuate in fully private contexts or settings that disavow egalitarian norms. We flag organizational DEI climate as a scope condition for future research in the General Discussion.
>
> **5.** In response to Reviewer 1, we have modernized the references that anchor our theoretical framing in both the Introduction and General Discussion, drawing on more contemporary feedback, goal‑setting, and social‑norms work alongside the foundational citations we retain.
