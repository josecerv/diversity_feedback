--- Footnote kix.fn3 ---
As discussed by Angrist and Pischke (2009) and Gomila (2021), OLS regressions are unbiased, and often preferable to logistic regressions thanks to their interpretability when estimating causal effects in experiments with binary outcomes, which is why we pre-registered employing them as our primary analysis strategy across all studies in this manuscript. However, all of our reported findings are meaningfully unchanged if we reanalyze our data using logistic regressions (see Appendix Tables S1-S6 for replications of all primary findings with logistic regressions).

--- Footnote kix.fn11 ---
Although our pre-registration specified that this model would include an indicator for the women underrepresented condition (women underrepresented = 1, women overrepresented = 0), for ease of interpretation we flipped this indicator to encode the women overrepresented condition as 1 and the women underrepresented condition as 0. --- Footnote kix.fn12 ---
To test whether treatment effects were equal in magnitude between the two candidate pools, we estimated separate OLS regression models predicting whether the final panelist selected was a woman, and in each model our primary predictor was an indicator for assignment to the gender feedback condition. The first model used only data from the women underrepresented condition (see Table 5, Model 1) and the second model used only data from the women overrepresented condition (see Table 5, Model 3). We then conducted a Wald test of H0: B_gender feedback (Model 1) + B_gender feedback (Model 3) = 0 to determine whether the treatment effects were equal in magnitude across the women overrepresented and women underrepresented conditions of our experiment from Study 5.


--- Footnote kix.fn13 ---
Studies 1 and 5 were excluded from this benchmarking analysis. In Study 1, participants did not make initial selections; they received feedback on hypothetical prior choices, making a quota rule inapplicable. In Study 5, our 2x2 design manipulated the initial candidate pool’s composition (varying whether women were underrepresented vs. overrepresented in the pool); because only half of the data from this study would be suitable for inclusion in this analysis, we have omitted Study 5 from this benchmarking exercise.

--- Footnote kix.sbd52qc7gtyl ---
To address the possibility that comparison-attribute feedback has weaker effects simply because participants are already using these attributes appropriately in their initial selections, we conducted a cross-study analysis pooling Studies 2, 3A, 3B, and 4B and restricting attention to participants whose initial selections contained zero of a given attribute. Among this conservative subset, where appropriate prior use cannot account for any non-response to feedback, race or gender feedback raised the likelihood of selecting a woman or racial by 22.50 percentage points (Woman: +27.59 pp; Racial Minority: +9.77; p < .001, N = 864)., whereas comparison-attribute feedback shifted subsequent selections by only +7.25 percentage points (p = .19, N = 414); a Wald test confirms the difference between the two pooled coefficients is significant (p = .016). Full per-cell results are reported in Appendix Section S[X].
