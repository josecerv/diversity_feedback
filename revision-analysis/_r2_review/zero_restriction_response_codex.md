# Zero-restriction response, Codex consolidated draft

## 1. Recommended response-letter prose

RESPONSE: Thank you for raising this alternative. We have responded in two ways. First, we have added new prose to the manuscript that makes explicit an assumption that was implicit in the prior draft: our account does not depend on participants’ initial selections being biased relative to some “appropriate” level for race, gender, or any comparison attribute. Second, we report a follow-up analysis below that attempts to address the alternative directly by holding the initial selection level constant.

Added to the manuscript in the Introduction (p. X), with a related footnote in the Discussion of Study 4 (p. X). In the revised Introduction, we now clarify that descriptive feedback is predicted to shift subsequent behavior on a dimension when that dimension carries an implicit injunctive norm against which past selections can be evaluated. We argue that race and gender are more likely to carry such a norm because anti-discrimination law, organizational policy, and social and reputational pressures make prejudiced selection normatively consequential. By contrast, the comparison attributes we test, such as film budget, expert affiliation, and geographic origin, do not typically carry a comparably entrenched injunctive norm. Thus, the theory does not require assuming that the comparison attributes were already being selected at “appropriate” levels initially. It predicts that descriptive feedback alone should be less likely to move subsequent selections on those dimensions because the same implicit injunctive standard is not present. We had treated this assumption as part of the theoretical logic in the prior draft, but we had not stated it directly. We have now revised the manuscript to make this scope condition explicit.

The additional analysis below then asks whether the alternative can explain the empirical pattern when the starting point is held constant. Although we cannot directly observe each participant’s perceived “appropriate” level for a given attribute, we can restrict the analysis to participants whose initial selections contained zero of the focal attribute. If feedback simply moved selections whenever the focal attribute began below an appropriate level, then feedback should move both race and gender selections and comparison-attribute selections in this subset. As Table R1 shows, however, race and gender feedback continues to shift subsequent selections in this restricted sample, whereas comparison-attribute feedback does not reliably do so.

## 2. Recommended manuscript-side prose

Placement: hybrid, with the main theoretical prose in the Introduction and a short empirical footnote in the Discussion of Study 4.

Introduction insertion:

Importantly, this account does not require assuming that participants’ initial selections are biased relative to some “appropriate” level for any attribute. We assume only that descriptive feedback shifts subsequent behavior on a dimension when that dimension carries an implicit injunctive norm against which past selections can be evaluated. Race and gender are more likely to carry such a standard because anti-discrimination law, organizational policy, and social and reputational pressures make prejudiced selection normatively consequential. By contrast, the comparison attributes we test, such as film budget, expert affiliation, or geographic origin, do not typically carry a comparably entrenched injunctive norm. We therefore do not predict descriptive feedback alone to shift later selections on those dimensions simply because participants initially selected low levels of the attribute.

Study 4 Discussion footnote:

In a follow-up pooled analysis reported in the response letter, we restricted the data to participant observations in which the initial selection contained zero of the focal attribute. Among participants who initially selected zero women or racial minorities, race and gender feedback increased the probability of selecting a woman or racial minority on the final selection by 21.28 percentage points (p < .001, N = 1,222). Among participants who initially selected zero individuals with the comparison attribute, comparison-attribute feedback increased final selection by 6.83 percentage points, which was not statistically significant (p = .21, N = 429). A Wald test comparing the two pooled coefficients was significant (difference = 14.46 percentage points, p = .016). This analysis is imperfect because zero may sometimes reflect a deliberate preference and the comparison-attribute subset is meaningfully underpowered, but the pattern provides suggestive evidence against the possibility that comparison-attribute feedback fails to move selections only because those attributes were already being selected at appropriate levels.

## 3. Rationale for placement

I recommend the hybrid placement. The Introduction should carry the main theoretical clarification because the point is a scope condition of the theory, not just an interpretation of Study 4. Putting the clarification there also lets the response letter say plainly that the paper now makes the assumption explicit.

The Study 4 footnote is still useful, but it should be narrower. It can summarize the new zero-restriction analysis at the point in the paper where Study 4b already bears most directly on the race and gender versus comparison-attribute distinction. That avoids burying the theoretical premise in a study-specific footnote while still giving the new analysis a manuscript-side home.

I would not put the main clarification only in the General Discussion. That placement is defensible, but it risks reading as a retrospective qualification after the empirical results rather than as part of the theory being tested.
