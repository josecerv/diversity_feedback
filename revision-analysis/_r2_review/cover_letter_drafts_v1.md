## A. Cover-letter summary page (to insert before "DEPARTMENT EDITOR'S COMMENTS TO AUTHORS:")

> [Date]
>
> George Wu, Department Editor, *Management Science*
>
> Re: MS‑BDE‑2025‑01479, "Does Feedback Enhance Diversity in Selection Decisions?"
>
> Dear Dr. Wu,
>
> We thank you for handling our revised manuscript and for the two reviewers' continued engagement with the paper. We very much appreciate the opportunity to address your remaining requests and the small comments raised by Reviewers 1 and 2. We hope you will agree that the changes summarized below have strengthened the paper further.
>
> Here is a summary of the most notable changes we have made in this revision:
>
> We have modernized the references that anchor our theoretical framing in response to Reviewer 1's request for more contemporary citations. Both the Introduction and General Discussion now draw on more recent feedback, goal‑setting, and social‑norms work (e.g., Locke & Latham, 2019; Itzchakov & Latham, 2018; Chen, Latham, & Piccolo, 2020; Schultz, Nolan, & Cialdini, 2018; Devine & Ash, 2022; Andor, Gerster, & Peters, 2020; Henry, Ferraro, & Kontoleon, 2019; Mertens & Schultz, 2021), alongside the foundational citations we retain.
>
> We have sharpened our theoretical framing of why purely descriptive feedback can change behavior in our domain even without a paired explicit goal. Consistent with classical Feedback Intervention Theory, feedback regulates behavior only when past performance can be evaluated against a standard. We now make explicit that this standard need not be supplied by an explicit goal when an implicit injunctive norm is already entrenched, as is the case for race and gender, where anti‑discrimination law, organizational policy, social and reputational pressures, and selectors' personally held egalitarian commitments jointly sustain a strong norm against prejudiced selection.
>
> We have situated the paper in the current sociopolitical landscape around DEI, in response to Reviewer 2's concern that the prior version read as pre‑2024. We now acknowledge the recent U.S. retreat from explicit DEI rhetoric while documenting three countervailing patterns that keep our central question relevant: (a) expanded international transparency requirements (Australia's Workplace Gender Equality Amendment Act 2023, the EU Pay Transparency Directive 2023/970, and the EU Women on Boards Directive 2022/2381); (b) continued U.S. public and stakeholder support for corporate DEI (e.g., the 2025 Bentley/Gallup survey reporting majority public support, and recent defeats of anti‑DEI shareholder proposals at Costco, Apple, Levi's, John Deere, and Goldman Sachs); and (c) firm‑level rebranding of diversity work (toward "belonging," "inclusive culture," and related framings) rather than wholesale dismantling.
>
> We have tightened the scope of our theoretical claims and articulated more explicit boundary conditions for when our effect should and should not generalize, in response to Reviewer 2. We now distinguish two pathways through which descriptive feedback shifts selection decisions: an external motivation to respond without prejudice, sustained by anti‑discrimination law, the observability of selection decisions, and broader stakeholder pressures that operate well beyond any single firm's stated DEI commitments; and an internal motivation to respond without prejudice, rooted in selectors' personally held egalitarian self‑image, which should be less malleable to changes in organizational climate. We expect our effect to generalize wherever selections are observable and selectors hold at least some egalitarian commitments, and to attenuate in fully private or anonymous selection contexts and in organizational settings that explicitly disavow egalitarian norms. We now flag organizational DEI climate as a scope condition and direction for future research in the General Discussion, alongside our existing discussions of public versus private feedback delivery and attribute visibility.
>
> We have addressed your two empirical requests. First, the new Study 1 importance post‑test (N = 300; one comparison attribute rated per participant) confirms that differences in perceived attribute importance cannot account for why only gender feedback moved subsequent selections: two of the three comparison attributes were rated significantly more important than the gender attribute, and the third did not differ in rated importance. Second, to address the alternative that comparison attributes are simply being selected at appropriate levels in participants' initial selections, we report a new pooled analysis (response‑letter Table R1) restricted to participants whose initial selections contained zero of the focal attribute, pooling across Studies 2, 3A, 3B, 4A, and 4B with participant‑clustered standard errors. Race and gender feedback raises the probability of selecting a woman or racial minority by +21.28 percentage points (p < .001, N = 1,222) in this restricted subset, while comparison‑attribute feedback raises it by only +6.83 percentage points (p = .21, N = 429); the Wald‑test difference is +14.46 pp (p = .016).
>
> We have streamlined the empirical sections of the paper following your guidance on length and repetition. The revised manuscript introduces a new consolidated, cross‑study Table 2 summarizing the secondary feedback‑about‑race/gender‑versus‑other‑attributes comparisons in a single location; relegates the regression specifications underlying those comparisons to a dedicated Appendix section (with explicit cross‑references from each Results section); and trims redundant Methods and Results language across later studies.
>
> Below we reproduce your letter and each of the reviews in their entirety, and we respond to every point made. We have provided normal text from the review team and our responses appear in bolded font for ease of differentiation.
>
> Thank you again for giving us the opportunity to revise our manuscript.

## B. Redrafted in-body 4-point summary to George Wu (replaces lines 14-18 of the current R2 doc)

> RESPONSE: Thank you for providing such clear guidance regarding the next round of review. We have carefully addressed each of the points you raise here. Detailed responses appear in the point‑by‑point replies to reviewers below, but to summarize:
>
> We have updated the references that Reviewer 1 flagged as dated. Both the Introduction and General Discussion now draw on more contemporary work in the feedback, goal‑setting, and social‑norms literatures (e.g., Locke & Latham, 2019; Itzchakov & Latham, 2018; Chen, Latham, & Piccolo, 2020; Schultz, Nolan, & Cialdini, 2018; Devine & Ash, 2022; Andor, Gerster, & Peters, 2020; Henry, Ferraro, & Kontoleon, 2019; Mertens & Schultz, 2021), alongside the foundational citations we retain (e.g., Kluger & DeNisi, 1996; Schultz et al., 2007).
>
> Relatedly, we have expanded the Introduction's theoretical framing (pp. X‑X) to articulate why descriptive feedback alone is sufficient to change selection decisions pertaining to women and underrepresented minorities. Consistent with the broader logic of the feedback literature, feedback regulates behavior only when there is a standard against which past performance can be evaluated. However, we now make clear that descriptive feedback can also be effective without a paired explicit goal in domains where a strong implicit standard is already entrenched. This is the case for the types of diversity‑related selection decisions we study, where injunctive norms against prejudicial behavior are sustained by anti‑discrimination law, organizational policy, social and reputational pressures, and selectors' personally held egalitarian commitments.
>
> We have revised the Introduction (pp. X) and General Discussion to situate the paper within the current sociopolitical landscape around DEI, in response to Reviewer 2's concern that the prior version read as pre‑2024. We acknowledge the recent U.S. retreat from explicit DEI rhetoric while documenting three countervailing patterns that reinforce the contemporary relevance of our research question. First, international regulatory demand for transparency around demographic representation continues to expand: Australia's Workplace Gender Equality Amendment (Closing the Gender Pay Gap) Act 2023 mandates public reporting of firm‑level gender representation and pay‑gap data (Ressia, 2024); the EU Pay Transparency Directive 2023/970 requires member‑state transposition by June 2026 (Mignano, 2024); and the EU Women on Boards Directive 2022/2381 establishes a 40% representation objective for the underrepresented gender on listed‑company boards. Second, within the U.S., public and stakeholder sentiment has shifted more slowly than corporate rhetoric, with a 2025 Bentley/Gallup survey reporting that 69% of U.S. adults believe businesses should promote DEI (Bentley/Gallup, 2025), and anti‑DEI shareholder proposals failing at numerous prominent organizations including Costco, Apple, Levi's, John Deere, and Goldman Sachs (CBS, 2025; Marketplace, 2025). Third, many firms appear to have rebranded their diversity‑related work, migrating toward "belonging," "inclusive culture," "respect," and "fairness" framings rather than dismantling it entirely (CNBC, 2025; HRGrapevine, 2025; Paradigm, 2025). Together, these developments helped us sharpen why our central question remains important: descriptive feedback about past selection patterns can change subsequent decisions when those patterns raise concerns about appearing prejudiced or failing to live up to one's own egalitarian standards, and these concerns persist even where corporate DEI language has receded.
>
> We have provided a more tightly bounded theoretical discussion of the scope of our intervention, in line with Reviewer 2's request. We agree that motivation to respond without prejudice is central to our account, and we now distinguish two pathways through which descriptive feedback can shift selection decisions. The external motivation pathway is sustained not only by a firm's own DEI commitments but also by the fact that selection decisions are typically observable to colleagues, candidates, supervisors, and other stakeholders, and by an implicit injunctive norm against prejudicial behavior that is anchored in anti‑discrimination law, employee and customer scrutiny, and broader reputational concerns. A given firm's retreat from DEI rhetoric therefore reflects shifting external pressure on the firm, not the disappearance of broader social pressure on individual selection decisions (Levi & Fried, 2024). The internal motivation pathway, by contrast, is rooted in selectors' personally held egalitarian self‑image and should be relatively insensitive to organizational climate; it is also the pathway most directly supported by Study 4's private‑feedback paradigm and mediation evidence. We expect our effect to generalize wherever selections are observable and selectors hold at least some egalitarian commitments, and to be attenuated in fully private or anonymous selection contexts and in organizational settings that explicitly disavow egalitarian norms. We now flag organizational DEI climate as a scope condition and direction for future research in the General Discussion's Future Directions and Limitations section, alongside our existing discussions of public versus private feedback delivery, attribute visibility and implicit injunctive norms, and additional plausible mechanisms (e.g., fairness norms surfaced in Study 5's mechanism analysis) that descriptive feedback may also activate.

## C. Updated point 4 (in cover letter and in-body summary) + new General Discussion insert

The updated point 4 above already reiterates that organizational DEI climate is now flagged as a scope condition and direction for future research in the GD. To make that claim verifiable, the GD's *Future Directions and Limitations* section needs a short corresponding paragraph. Proposed insert (to follow the existing private/public‑delivery paragraph in the GD):

> Relatedly, our studies hold the broader organizational climate around DEI roughly constant: participants made selection decisions outside of any specific firm and without exposure to organization‑level cues for or against egalitarian commitments. We theorize that the external motivation to respond without prejudice is sustained largely by anti‑discrimination law, the observability of selection decisions, and broader societal norms that operate well beyond any one firm's stated DEI commitments, while the internal motivation pathway should be relatively insensitive to organizational climate. Future research could test these predictions directly by varying the organizational context in which descriptive feedback is delivered, including settings that have explicitly retreated from DEI rhetoric or that frame selection as a "merit‑only" enterprise, to map how variation in firm‑level climate moderates the magnitude of descriptive feedback's effect on subsequent selection decisions.
