> May X, 2026
>
> George Wu, Department Editor, *Management Science*
>
> Re: MS‑BDE‑2025‑01479, "Does Feedback Enhance Diversity in Selection Decisions?"
>
> Dear Dr. Wu,
>
> We thank you for handling our revised manuscript and for the two reviewers' continued engagement with the paper. We very much appreciate the opportunity to address your remaining requests and the small comments raised by Reviewers 1 and 2. We hope you will agree that the changes summarized below have strengthened the paper further.
>
> Here is a summary of the most notable changes we have made in this revision:
>
> We have modernized the references that anchor our theoretical framing in response to Reviewer 1's request for more contemporary citations. Both the Introduction and General Discussion now draw on more recent feedback, goal‑setting, and social‑norms work (e.g., Locke & Latham, 2019; Itzchakov & Latham, 2018; Chen, Latham, & Piccolo, 2020; Schultz, Nolan, & Cialdini, 2018; Devine & Ash, 2022; Andor, Gerster, & Peters, 2020; Henry, Ferraro, & Kontoleon, 2019; Mertens & Schultz, 2021), alongside the foundational citations we retain.
>
> We have sharpened our theoretical framing of why purely descriptive feedback can change behavior in our domain even without a paired explicit goal. Consistent with classical Feedback Intervention Theory, feedback regulates behavior only when past performance can be evaluated against a standard. We now make explicit that this standard need not be supplied by an explicit goal when an implicit injunctive norm is already entrenched, as is the case for race and gender, where anti‑discrimination law, organizational policy, social and reputational pressures, and selectors' personally held egalitarian commitments jointly sustain a strong norm against prejudiced selection.
>
> We have situated the paper in the current sociopolitical landscape around DEI, in response to Reviewer 2's concern that the prior version read as pre‑2024. We now acknowledge the recent U.S. retreat from explicit DEI rhetoric while documenting three countervailing patterns that keep our central question relevant: (a) expanded international transparency requirements (Australia's Workplace Gender Equality Amendment Act 2023, the EU Pay Transparency Directive 2023/970, and the EU Women on Boards Directive 2022/2381); (b) continued U.S. public and stakeholder support for corporate DEI (e.g., the 2025 Bentley/Gallup survey reporting majority public support, and recent defeats of anti‑DEI shareholder proposals at Costco, Apple, Levi's, John Deere, and Goldman Sachs); and (c) firm‑level rebranding of diversity work (toward "belonging," "inclusive culture," and related framings) rather than wholesale dismantling.
>
> We have tightened the scope of our theoretical claims and articulated more explicit boundary conditions for when our effect should and should not generalize, in response to Reviewer 2. We now distinguish two pathways through which descriptive feedback shifts selection decisions: an external motivation to respond without prejudice, sustained by anti‑discrimination law, the observability of selection decisions, and broader stakeholder pressures that operate well beyond any single firm's stated DEI commitments; and an internal motivation to respond without prejudice, rooted in selectors' personally held egalitarian self‑image, which should be less malleable to changes in organizational climate. We expect our effect to generalize wherever selections are observable and selectors hold at least some egalitarian commitments, and to attenuate in fully private or anonymous selection contexts and in organizational settings that explicitly disavow egalitarian norms. We now flag organizational DEI climate as a scope condition and direction for future research in the General Discussion, alongside our existing discussions of public versus private feedback delivery and attribute visibility.
>
> We have addressed your two empirical requests. First, the new Study 1 importance post‑test (N = 300) confirms that differences in perceived attribute importance cannot explain why only gender feedback moved subsequent selections: two of the three comparison attributes were rated significantly more important than gender, and the third did not differ. Second, to address the alternative that comparison attributes are already selected at appropriate levels initially, we restrict to participants whose initial selections contained zero of the focal attribute and report a new pooled analysis across Studies 2, 3A, 3B, 4A, and 4B (response‑letter Table R1). Zero is a defensible benchmark precisely because we cannot observe each participant's private notion of "appropriate": anyone whose initial selection contained zero of the focal attribute is necessarily below any plausible such benchmark. If feedback merely corrected under‑selection relative to a private benchmark, it should move participants for both race/gender and comparison attributes in this subset. Instead, only race and gender feedback does (+21.28 pp, p < .001, N = 1,222), while comparison‑attribute feedback does not (+6.83 pp, p = .21, N = 429); the Wald‑test difference is +14.46 pp (p = .016).
>
> We have streamlined the empirical sections of the paper following your guidance on length and repetition. The revised manuscript introduces a new consolidated, cross‑study Table 2 summarizing the secondary feedback‑about‑race/gender‑versus‑other‑attributes comparisons in a single location; relegates the regression specifications underlying those comparisons to a dedicated Appendix section (with explicit cross‑references from each Results section); and trims redundant Methods and Results language across later studies.
>
> Below we reproduce your letter and each of the reviews in their entirety, and we respond to every point made. We have provided normal text from the review team and our responses appear in bolded font for ease of differentiation.
>
> Thank you again for giving us the opportunity to revise our manuscript.

> Relatedly, our studies hold the broader organizational climate around DEI roughly constant: participants made selection decisions outside of any specific firm and without exposure to organization‑level cues for or against egalitarian commitments. We theorize that the external motivation to respond without prejudice is sustained largely by anti‑discrimination law, the observability of selection decisions, and broader societal norms that operate well beyond any one firm's stated DEI commitments, while the internal motivation pathway should be relatively insensitive to organizational climate. Future research could test these predictions directly by varying the organizational context in which descriptive feedback is delivered, including settings that have explicitly retreated from DEI rhetoric or that frame selection as a "merit‑only" enterprise, to map how variation in firm‑level climate moderates the magnitude of descriptive feedback's effect on subsequent selection decisions.
