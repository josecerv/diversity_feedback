## Draft 2

Locator: New Introduction paragraph immediately before “We propose that when descriptive feedback reveals that an evaluator’s past selections included few women or racial minorities...”.

> This logic implies a boundary condition on when descriptive feedback alone should motivate change. The classical feedback literature establishes that pairing feedback with an explicit goal can work because it supplies a standard against which past performance can be evaluated. However, an explicit goal should be less necessary when a strong implicit standard is already entrenched. Diversity-related selection decisions provide a clean test of this claim because anti-discrimination law, social and reputational pressures to avoid appearing prejudiced, and selectors’ personally held egalitarian self-image jointly sustain an entrenched implicit injunctive norm against prejudiced selection (Plant & Devine, 1998; Crandall & Eshleman, 2003; Álvarez-Benjumea, 2023). When descriptive feedback reveals a discrepancy between that implicit standard and past behavior, we theorize that motivation to respond without prejudice will be activated even in the absence of a specific external goal.

Why this wording: This paragraph closely follows the response letter’s theoretical promise while making the standard-setting logic explicit. It positions diversity-related selection as the clean test case rather than making a broad claim about all descriptive feedback.

## Draft 4

Locator: General Discussion, Theoretical and Practical Contributions section, after the sentence ending “an implicit injunctive norm to avoid prejudice has already been internalized by the decision-maker.”

> Our account also clarifies two forms of motivation through which descriptive feedback can change selection decisions. External motivation to control prejudice should be strongest when selection decisions are observable to colleagues, prospective candidates, supervisors, customers, employees, or outside stakeholders, and when evaluators face legal accountability or reputational costs for choices that appear exclusionary. In this sense, firms’ public DEI rhetoric is better understood as one downstream signal of broader legal, employee, customer, and reputational pressures, rather than the source of the implicit injunctive norm itself (Levi & Fried, 2024). Internal motivation to control prejudice, by contrast, reflects selectors’ personally held egalitarian beliefs and their desire to view themselves as non-prejudiced decision makers. This latter mechanism may be less sensitive to a given organization’s current DEI rhetoric, and it is the pathway most directly supported by Study 4, where feedback was delivered privately and internal motivation to respond without prejudice mediated subsequent selections. Study 4B’s political-ideology null is consistent with this broader account, suggesting that external motivation to control prejudice and internal motivation to control prejudice may both operate across the political spectrum.

Why this wording: The paragraph separates external and internal motivation without treating firm rhetoric as the cause of the norm. The final sentence incorporates the requested political-ideology connection while keeping the interpretation conservative.

> To rule out the possibility that Study 1’s pattern was driven by participants viewing gender as more important than the comparison attributes, we conducted a separate pretest. We recruited N = 300 Prolific participants in a between-subjects design in which each participant rated one expert attribute on a 1 to 7 importance scale, with the NPR producer framing held constant. Participants rated whether a guest was a woman as moderately important (M = 3.64, SD = 1.76, n = 76). By comparison, participants rated “worked at a university” as more important (M = 4.67, SD = 1.60; t(148.8) = -3.76, p < .001, d = -0.61) and “were under 50 years old” as more important (M = 4.44, SD = 1.73; t(149.0) = -2.81, p = .006, d = -0.46), while “based on the West Coast of the United States” did not differ significantly in importance (M = 3.37, SD = 1.75; t(149.0) = 0.95, p = .343, d = +0.16). These results make Study 1 a conservative test: the comparison attributes were rated as equally or more important than gender, so differences in perceived importance cannot explain why only gender feedback moved subsequent selections.

Why this wording: The paragraph uses the response letter’s updated pretest numbers and frames the test conservatively. It directly addresses the reviewer’s concern that the comparison attributes might have been less important than gender.
