## 3. APPENDIX NOTE

Exact insertion point: create a new Appendix section, preferably after the existing Study 4 comparison-attribute material or after the appendix table that reports the race and gender versus comparison-attribute regressions. Suggested title: “Appendix Section S[X]: Zero-Baseline Analysis of Initial Nonselection.”

Appendix paragraph text:

To address the possibility that descriptive feedback about comparison attributes had weaker effects because participants had already represented those attributes appropriately in their initial selections, we conducted a pooled zero-baseline analysis across Studies 2, 3A, 3B, and 4B. We excluded Study 4A because no participant began with zero of any comparison attribute. The analysis restricted the sample to participant-attribute observations in which a participant’s initial selections contained zero options with the focal attribute, and clustered standard errors by participant. Selecting zero options with an attribute provides a conservative signal of underrepresentation that should leave room for descriptive feedback to affect a later selection regardless of which attribute is summarized.

Among participants who initially selected zero women or racial minorities, race or gender feedback increased the probability of subsequently selecting a woman or racial minority by 22.50 percentage points (*p* < .001, *N* = 864). By contrast, among participants who initially selected zero options with one of the comparison attributes, feedback about that attribute did not significantly increase the probability of subsequently selecting an option with that attribute (effect = +7.25 percentage points, *p* = .19, *N* = 414). A Wald test confirmed that the pooled race and gender coefficient was significantly larger than the pooled comparison-attribute coefficient (*p* = .016, 95% CI [+2.87, +27.63]). The accompanying table should report the coefficient, standard error, confidence interval, *p* value, and *N* for the race and gender zero-baseline estimate, the comparison-attribute zero-baseline estimate, and the Wald difference test.

## 4. RESPONSE LETTER TEXT

Replace the current response to the DE’s zero-baseline question with:

Thank you for raising this possibility. We now address it in two places. First, in the Study 4 preamble, where the manuscript already discusses whether comparison attributes can rule out alternative explanations for our findings, we added text explicitly considering the possibility that participants may have already used those attributes appropriately in their initial selections. Second, we report a new pooled zero-baseline analysis in Appendix Section S[X]. Because this analysis pools across Study 4B, we refer readers to the Appendix from the Study 4 preamble rather than reporting those coefficients before Study 4B Results.

Our logic was that selecting zero options with a given attribute provides a conservative signal of underrepresentation. If feedback simply corrects any initially inappropriate level of representation, then feedback about the complete absence of a comparison attribute should also shift subsequent selections. To examine this possibility, we pooled data from Studies 2, 3A, 3B, and 4B, excluding Study 4A because no participant in that study began with zero of any comparison attribute. Among participants who selected zero women or racial minorities initially, race or gender feedback significantly increased the probability of selecting a woman or racial minority as the final selection by 22.50 percentage points (*p* < .001, *N* = 864). By contrast, among participants who initially selected zero options with a comparison attribute, feedback about that attribute did not significantly increase the probability of selecting an option with the relevant attribute (effect = +7.25 percentage points, *p* = .19, *N* = 414). A Wald test confirmed that the pooled effect of feedback was significantly larger for race and gender than for comparison attributes (*p* = .016, 95% CI [+2.87, +27.63]).

These analyses cannot prove that participants never considered comparison attributes appropriately at the initial selection stage. However, they help rule out the stronger version of the alternative explanation. Even in cases where participants initially selected zero options with a comparison attribute, feedback about that absence did not produce the same pattern as feedback about the absence of women or racial minorities. Thus, the differential held even under a conservative zero-baseline test.
