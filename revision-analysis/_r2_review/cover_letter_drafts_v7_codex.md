> **1.** We have addressed your two empirical requests.
>
> First, the new Study 1 importance post-test (N = 300) confirms that the comparison attributes were not rated less important than gender, so importance differences cannot explain why only gender feedback moved selections.

Together, these results strengthen the theoretical point: race and gender feedback appears to move selections because those dimensions carry an implicit injunctive norm against prejudiced selection, not simply because they start out under-selected.

> **2.** We have streamlined the empirical sections of the paper following your guidance on length and repetition. The revised manuscript is now [X] pages and reflects a substantial streamlining of the Methods and Results sections, particularly for later studies to avoid unnecessary repetition and redundancy.

> **3.** In response to Reviewer 2, we have updated the Introduction’s discussion of the current landscape around DEI and used that update to sharpen our theoretical claims. We acknowledge that U.S. support for new DEI initiatives has retreated since 2024, while noting three parallel patterns that keep our central question relevant: international transparency requirements continue to expand, U.S. public and stakeholder support for corporate DEI remains broadly intact, and many firms have rebranded their diversity work rather than dismantling it. These patterns help clarify why the motivation to respond without prejudice that drives our effect can operate through two pathways: an external pathway, sustained by anti-discrimination law, the visibility of selection decisions, and broader public expectations, and an internal pathway, rooted in evaluators’ own desire to act consistently with egalitarian values. We therefore expect the effect to be strongest where selections are visible and evaluators care about acting and being seen as fair, and weakest in fully private settings or in organizations that openly reject egalitarian norms. The General Discussion now identifies organizational DEI climate as a condition that may limit the effect and should be tested in future research.
