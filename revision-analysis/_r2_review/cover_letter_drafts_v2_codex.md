We appreciate the opportunity to address your remaining requests as well as the small comments raised by Reviewers 1 and 2, and we have worked to strengthen the paper further in response.
>
> Here is a summary of the most notable changes we have made in this revision:
>
> We have updated the references that Reviewer 1 flagged as dated. The revised manuscript now draws on more contemporary work in the feedback, goal-setting, and social norms literatures alongside the foundational work that remains central to our theoretical framing.
>
> We have expanded the Introduction’s theoretical framing to clarify why purely descriptive feedback can change behavior in our domain even without a paired explicit goal. Consistent with Feedback Intervention Theory, feedback regulates behavior when past performance can be evaluated against a standard. We now make clearer that, in the race and gender selection decisions we study, that standard can come from an implicit injunctive norm against prejudicial selection, sustained by anti-discrimination law, organizational policy, social and reputational pressures, and selectors’ personally held egalitarian commitments.
>
> We have revised the Introduction and General Discussion to better situate the paper in the current sociopolitical landscape around DEI, in response to Reviewer 2’s concern that the prior version read as pre-2024. We now acknowledge the recent U.S. retreat from explicit DEI rhetoric while also discussing countervailing developments, including expanded international transparency requirements, continued U.S. public and stakeholder support for corporate DEI, and firm-level reframing of diversity work toward language such as belonging, inclusive culture, respect, and fairness.
>
> We have tightened the scope of our theoretical claims in response to Reviewer 2. We now distinguish two pathways through which descriptive feedback may shift selection decisions: an external motivation to respond without prejudice, shaped by anti-discrimination law, the observability of selection decisions, and broader stakeholder pressures that can operate beyond any single firm’s stated DEI commitments; and an internal motivation to respond without prejudice, rooted in selectors’ personally held egalitarian self-image, which may be less sensitive to changes in organizational climate. We now discuss organizational DEI climate as a scope condition and direction for future research in the General Discussion, alongside our existing discussions of public versus private feedback delivery, attribute visibility, and other mechanisms descriptive feedback may activate.
>
> We have also addressed your two empirical requests. First, the new Study 1 importance post-test (N = 300; one comparison attribute rated per participant) suggests that differences in perceived attribute importance cannot account for why only gender feedback moved subsequent selections: two of the three comparison attributes were rated significantly more important than the gender attribute, and the third did not differ significantly in rated importance. Second, to address the alternative that comparison attributes are simply being selected at appropriate levels in participants’ initial selections, we report a new pooled analysis restricted to participants whose initial selections contained zero of the focal attribute. Pooling across Studies 2, 3A, 3B, 4A, and 4B with participant-clustered standard errors, race and gender feedback raises the probability of selecting a woman or racial minority by +21.28 percentage points (p < .001, N = 1,222) in this restricted subset, while comparison-attribute feedback raises it by +6.83 percentage points (p = .21, N = 429). The Wald-test difference is +14.46 percentage points (p = .016).
>
> Finally, we have streamlined the empirical sections of the paper following your guidance on length and repetition. The revised manuscript introduces a new consolidated, cross-study Table 2 summarizing the secondary comparisons of feedback about race and gender versus feedback about other attributes in one location; moves the regression specifications underlying those comparisons to a dedicated Appendix section with explicit cross-references from each Results section; and trims redundant Methods and Results language across later studies.
>
> Below we reproduce your letter and each of the reviews in their entirety, and we respond to every point made. We have provided normal text from the review team and our responses appear in bolded font for ease of differentiation.
>
> Thank you again for giving us the opportunity to revise our manuscript.

## B. Redrafted in-body 4-point summary to George Wu (replaces lines 14-18 of the current R2 doc)

> RESPONSE: Thank you for providing such clear guidance regarding the next round of review. We have carefully addressed each of the points you raise here. Detailed responses appear in the point-by-point replies to reviewers below, but to summarize:
>
> We have updated the references that Reviewer 1 flagged as dated. The Introduction and General Discussion now draw on more contemporary work in the feedback, goal-setting, and social norms literatures, including Locke and Latham (2019), Itzchakov and Latham (2018), Chen, Latham, and Piccolo (2020), Schultz, Nolan, and Cialdini (2018), Devine and Ash (2022), Andor, Gerster, and Peters (2020), Henry, Ferraro, and Kontoleon (2019), and Mertens and Schultz (2021), alongside the foundational citations we retain.
>
> Relatedly, we have expanded the Introduction’s theoretical framing (pp. X-X) to articulate why descriptive feedback alone can be sufficient to change selection decisions pertaining to women and underrepresented racial minorities. Consistent with the broader logic of the feedback literature, feedback regulates behavior only when there is a standard against which past performance can be evaluated. We now make clearer that descriptive feedback can also be effective without a paired explicit goal in domains where a strong implicit standard is already entrenched. This is the case for the types of diversity-related selection decisions we study, where the implicit injunctive norm against prejudicial behavior is sustained by anti-discrimination law, organizational policy, social and reputational pressures, and selectors’ personally held egalitarian commitments.
>
> We have revised the Introduction (p. X) and General Discussion to acknowledge the shifting sociopolitical landscape around organizational commitments to DEI in the United States, in response to Reviewer 2’s concern that the prior version read as pre-2024. We now describe the recent U.S. retreat from explicit DEI rhetoric while also discussing three countervailing patterns that help explain why the paper’s central question remains relevant. First, international regulatory demand for transparency around demographic representation continues to expand, including Australia’s Workplace Gender Equality Amendment (Closing the Gender Pay Gap) Act 2023, the EU Pay Transparency Directive 2023/970, and the EU Women on Boards Directive 2022/2381. Second, within the U.S., public and stakeholder sentiment has shifted more slowly than some corporate and legal rhetoric: a 2025 Bentley/Gallup survey reports that 69% of U.S. adults believe businesses should promote DEI, and anti-DEI shareholder proposals have failed at prominent organizations including Costco, Apple, Levi’s, John Deere, Goldman Sachs, and others. Third, many firms appear to have reframed or rebranded diversity-related work rather than dismantling it entirely, moving toward language such as belonging, inclusive culture, respect, fairness, and inclusive experiences. Together, these developments helped us clarify that the recent pullback from explicit DEI rhetoric is relevant to the scope of our theory, but does not remove all of the legal, social, reputational, and self-image concerns that can make descriptive feedback about race and gender selection patterns consequential.
>
> We have provided a more tightly bounded theoretical discussion of the scope of our intervention, in line with Reviewer 2’s request. We agree that motivation to respond without prejudice is central to our account of increased selections of women and racial minorities, and we now distinguish two pathways through which descriptive feedback can shift selection decisions. The external motivation pathway is shaped by a firm’s norms, but also by the fact that selection decisions are often observable to colleagues, candidates, supervisors, and other stakeholders, and by an implicit injunctive norm against prejudicial behavior that is anchored in anti-discrimination law, employee and customer scrutiny, and broader reputational concerns. A given firm’s retreat from DEI rhetoric therefore reflects shifting external pressure on the firm, not the disappearance of broader social pressure on individual selection decisions. The internal motivation pathway, by contrast, is rooted in selectors’ personally held egalitarian self-image and may be less malleable in the face of changes in organizational climate; it is also the pathway most directly supported by Study 4’s private-feedback paradigm and mediation evidence. We also note that Study 4B did not find significant moderation by political ideology or party affiliation, and we now discuss related field evidence from Kirgios et al. (2022) that is consistent with the possibility that these concerns can operate across partisan lines. We expect our effect to generalize wherever selections are observable and selectors hold at least some egalitarian commitments, and to be attenuated in fully private or anonymous selection contexts or in organizational settings where diversity-related concerns are explicitly rejected or devalued. Finally, we now clarify that motivation to respond without prejudice is one mechanism descriptive feedback can activate, but not the only possible mechanism. Study 5’s mechanism analysis suggests that fairness concerns can also shape responses to feedback in some settings, and we now flag additional mechanisms and organizational DEI climate as directions for future research in the General Discussion’s Future Directions and Limitations section.

Proposed General Discussion insert, to follow the existing private versus public delivery paragraph in the Future Directions and Limitations section:

> Relatedly, our studies hold the broader organizational climate around DEI relatively constant: participants made selection decisions outside of any specific firm and without exposure to organization-level cues for or against egalitarian commitments. We theorize that the external motivation to respond without prejudice is sustained not only by firm-level norms, but also by anti-discrimination law, the observability of selection decisions, and broader societal expectations that can operate beyond any one firm’s stated DEI commitments. By contrast, the internal motivation pathway should be less malleable in response to changes in organizational climate because it is rooted in selectors’ personally held egalitarian self-image. Future research could test these predictions directly by varying the organizational context in which descriptive feedback is delivered, including settings that have explicitly retreated from DEI rhetoric, settings that frame selection in more narrowly merit-focused terms, or settings that actively devalue diversity-related concerns. Such work would help clarify how variation in firm-level climate moderates the magnitude of descriptive feedback’s effect on subsequent selection decisions.
